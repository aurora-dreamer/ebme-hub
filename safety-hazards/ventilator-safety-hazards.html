§ 00 · Stakeholder Risk Map
The Ventilator
Risk Landscape
Mechanical ventilation is one of the highest-risk interventions in critical care. Failures — whether device, user, or service — carry direct life-threatening consequences with seconds to minutes for intervention.
Device Scope
ICU / Critical Care
Draeger · Hamilton · Maquet · Carestation
Full-featured, multiple modes (VC, PC, SIMV, PSV, APRV), invasive and NIV. Long-term intubated patients. Compressor + O₂ blending.
Transport Ventilator
Medumat · Hamilton T1 · LTV Series
Portable, battery-powered. Used during inter-hospital transfer or within hospital (CT, theatre). Simplified modes, turbine or pneumatic drive.
Emergency / MRI
MRI-compatible · Resuscitation
MRI-safe (no ferromagnetic components), pneumatically driven. Emergency: resuscitation rooms, ambulance. Robustness over feature-breadth.

§ 01 · Patient Hazards
Direct Physiological Risk
Patient harm from ventilator failure manifests within minutes. Lung-protective strategies (ARDS net) and closed-loop alarm monitoring form the primary defence line.
💥
P.01
Barotrauma / Volutrauma
CRITICAL
Cause
Ppeak too high (>40 cmH₂O); Vt too large (>10 mL/kg); inadvertent PEEP stacking; inappropriate mode for obstructive disease.
Harm
Pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum, tension pneumothorax — cardiac arrest within minutes. Alveolar rupture and VILI (ventilator-induced lung injury).
Magnitude
Immediate life-threat. ARDS lung protective: Vt 6 mL/kg IBW, Ppeak <30 cmH₂O, Pplat <28 cmH₂O.
Control
Ppeak alarm (hard limit), software pressure relief, dual-CPU watchdog, compliance monitoring, lung-protective protocol.
Standard
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.12.4 — Ppeak alarm mandatory
⚠️
P.02
Inadvertent Extubation / Circuit Disconnect
CRITICAL
Cause
Patient movement, unsecured ETT, heavy circuit tubing pulling, coughing during transport, inadequate tube fixation.
Harm
Complete cessation of ventilation → hypoxia → cardiac arrest within 3–5 minutes if undetected. Laryngeal trauma on extubation.
Detection
Low Ppeak alarm (disconnect = pressure drops); low minute volume alarm; ETCO₂ waveform disappearance (fastest); SpO₂ drop (delayed).
Control
Mandatory low airway pressure alarm (cannot be disabled per ISO 80601-2-12); ETCO₂ continuous monitoring; circuit supports and swivel connectors.
🫧
P.03
Hypoxia — O₂ Supply / Blending Failure
CRITICAL
Cause
Pipeline O₂ failure; blender valve fault; FiO₂ sensor drift (under-reads → operator increases FiO₂ delivery beyond intended); air compressor failure on air-oxygen blended ventilators.
Harm
SpO₂ desaturation → hypoxic brain injury. Critical in ICU patients with already compromised lungs.
Control
FiO₂ low alarm; independent SpO₂ monitor; O₂ analyser in inspiratory limb; O₂ supply pressure alarm; backup cylinder.
Standard
IEC 60601-1-8 · ISO 80601-2-12 §201.12.6
🔄
P.04
Patient–Ventilator Asynchrony
MAJOR
Cause
Trigger threshold too insensitive; inspiratory time mismatch; reverse triggering; mode inappropriate for respiratory drive. Auto-PEEP causing trigger failure.
Harm
Increased work of breathing, respiratory fatigue, barotrauma risk, patient distress, agitation-driven unsafe self-extubation.
Types
Missed triggers, double-triggering, delayed cycling, reverse triggering — visible on flow-time and pressure-time waveforms.
Control
Trigger sensitivity adjustment; mode selection; waveform monitoring; sedation review; NAVA (neurally adjusted ventilatory assist) on advanced platforms.
🦠
P.05
VAP — Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia
MAJOR
Cause
Contaminated breathing circuit; condensate pooling and retrograde aspiration; failure to use HME/heated humidifier appropriately; inadequate circuit change frequency.
Harm
VAP: mortality 20–50% in ICU. Adds 7–21 days of ventilation. High antibiotic burden.
Incidence
1–4 episodes per 1000 ventilator-days in well-managed ICUs. Device and care bundle dependent.
Control
Heat and moisture exchanger (HME) or closed heated humidifier; circuit change per protocol (not <48–72 hrs); drainage of condensate away from patient; subglottic suction ETT.
⚡
P.06
Electrical Micro-shock (CF Applied Part)
MAJOR
Cause
Leakage current via humidifier, heated wire circuit, or flow sensor to patient. Ground fault or insulation breakdown. Fluid ingress to circuit connections.
Harm
VF at ≥10 µA via intracardiac path (CF classification). Burn injury at contact points. Risk highest when invasive monitoring co-exists.
Control
CF applied part ≤10 µA leakage (normal), ≤50 µA (single fault); IEC 62353 annual re-test; Class I earthing; hospital-grade outlets; no extension leads.
Standard
IEC 60601-1 Table 1 · IEC 62353 recurrent
🔋
P.07
Power Failure — Battery Exhaustion
CRITICAL
Cause
Mains failure without UPS; transport battery not charged; battery degradation below spec (≥2 hr autonomy required); BMS fault preventing charge.
Harm
Complete ventilator shutdown → apnoea in a patient who may have no respiratory drive → hypoxic cardiac arrest.
Control
Battery capacity test at PPM; mains connect alarm; battery low and critical alarms; manual resuscitation bag (Ambu) always at bedside — last resort.
Standard
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.6.8.3 — min internal power source
🌬️
P.08
Rebreathing — CO₂ Retention
MAJOR
Cause
Exhaled valve malfunction; inappropriate ventilation mode with trapped gas; dead space too large; HME resistance too high in paediatric circuit.
Harm
Hypercapnia → respiratory acidosis → arrhythmia → cardiac arrest. Rising ETCO₂ is earliest sign.
Control
ETCO₂ monitoring (capnography); expiratory valve inspection at PPM; circuit configuration verification; high ETCO₂ alarm.
§ 02 · Clinical User Hazards
Use Error &
Human Factors
ICU nurses and RT staff interact with ventilators under high cognitive load. Complex interfaces, multiple simultaneous alarms, and critical patients create optimal conditions for use error.
⚙️
U.01
Incorrect Mode / Parameter Setting
CRITICAL
Cause
Wrong mode selected (VC when PS intended); Vt set per kg body weight not IBW; rate incorrectly set; PEEP removed inadvertently; I:E ratio inappropriate.
Harm
Barotrauma, hypo/hyperventilation, patient-ventilator asynchrony, VILI, respiratory alkalosis/acidosis.
Human factor
Different ventilator models on same ICU have different knob/button layouts and default values. Handover gaps. Cognitive overload at night.
Control
Standardised settings checklist at start of shift; independent double-check after any changes; IEC 62366 usability testing for interface design.
🔔
U.02
Alarm Fatigue
CRITICAL
Cause
ICU patients generate 350–700 alarms per bed per day. >90% are non-actionable. Nurses silence or disable alarms that have repeatedly cried wolf.
Harm
True critical alarms (disconnect, apnoea, high pressure) missed → patient death. ECRI Institute: alarm hazards #1 hospital technology hazard for 10+ consecutive years.
Control
IEC 60601-1-8 three-priority alarm; individualised alarm limits; smart alarm systems; mandatory minimum alarm settings per protocol; EBME role in reducing nuisance alarm rate.
Standard
IEC 60601-1-8 · AAMI/ECRI Alarm Safety white paper
🔀
U.03
Circuit Misconnection
MAJOR
Cause
Inspiratory and expiratory limbs swapped; wrong-size connector forced; nebuliser tubing in wrong port; PEEP valve not fitted correctly.
Harm
Reversed circuit = patient exhales into machine, CO₂ rebreathing; overpressure if expiratory port blocked; immediate hypercapnia.
Control
ISO 5356 standard connectors (22/15 mm asymmetric); colour-coded circuit limbs (white insp / grey exp); pre-use check; misconnection alarm on some platforms.
🚑
U.04
Inadequate Transport Preparation
MAJOR
Cause
Transport ventilator battery not charged; PEEP not maintained during switch; O₂ cylinder inadequate for journey time; alarm limits not rechecked on transport device.
Harm
Apnoea, desaturation, haemodynamic collapse during inter-hospital or intra-hospital transfer. High-risk environment (CT, MRI) with limited resuscitation access.
Control
Pre-transport checklist (SAFE-ICU protocol); battery >80% before every transport; cylinder duration calculation; manual Ambu bag at hand.
💧
U.05
Humidifier Burns / Water Ingress
MODERATE
Cause
Heated humidifier chamber empty → overheating → hot gas delivery; condensate in circuit water trap not drained → aspiration into patient.
Harm
Airway burns (hot gas); tracheobronchitis; aspiration pneumonia; water ingress to ventilator electronics causing short circuit.
Control
Auto-fill humidifier or dry-out alarm; heated wire circuit to prevent condensate; water traps drainage protocol; HME as alternative for shorter ventilation.
💉
U.06
Aerosol / Biological Exposure
MODERATE
Cause
Patient coughing during circuit disconnection; open suctioning without viral filter; aerosolising procedures (nebuliser, bronchoscopy).
Harm
Respiratory pathogen transmission (TB, COVID-19, influenza); occupational exposure to drug aerosols (inhaled antibiotics).
Control
Viral/bacterial filter on expiratory port; closed suctioning system; PPE (FFP3, visor); pause ventilation before disconnection; negative pressure room where applicable.
Regulation
COSHH 2002 · IPC guidance · NHS IPC manual
📵
U.07
Alarm Limit Not Set / Too Wide
CRITICAL
Cause
Alarm limits left at factory defaults (too wide); limits silenced for a procedure and not restored; low-priority alarms ignored because limits too tight triggering constantly.
Harm
Critical events (disconnect, high pressure, apnoea) fall within the alarm window and no alert fires → patient harmed before discovery.
Control
Mandatory alarm limits per clinical protocol; IEC 60601-1-8 mandated alarms cannot be disabled; shift checklist includes alarm limit verification.
🌐
U.08
EMI / Wireless Device Interference
MODERATE
Cause
Mobile phone too close; electrosurgery in adjacent bay; MRI stray field on incompatible ventilator; RFID reader interference.
Harm
False alarm cascade; ventilator mode reset; trigger sensitivity disruption; display freeze. Rare but documented.
Control
IEC 60601-1-2 EMC testing; respect separation distances; MRI-compatible ventilator in MRI suite; co-located device EMC risk assessment at installation.
§ 03 · Service Engineer Hazards
Occupational &
Patient-Forward Risk
EBME engineers carry dual liability — personal safety and patient safety through the quality of service. Post-service errors propagate directly into a life-critical device.
⚠
Regulatory context Post-service verification is mandatory under UK MDR 2002. Any service that could affect safety performance must be documented. Failure to verify performance after repair is a potential MHRA reportable event if patient harm results.
⚡
E.01
Electric Shock — Mains Voltage
CRITICAL
Cause
Live PCB fault-finding; probing energised PSU; capacitor discharge not waited out after mains removal; damaged test leads; no isolation.
Harm
230 V AC at >100 mA → VF → death. Flash burns. Muscle tetany preventing release. 50 mA can cause cardiac arrest.
Control
LOTO; isolation transformer for live work; Cat IV test leads per IEC 61010; competency sign-off; two-person rule for live mains probing; capacitor discharge wait (typically 60 sec).
Regulation
Electricity at Work Regs 1989 · IEC 61010-1
🩸
E.02
Biological Exposure — Patient Circuit
MAJOR
Cause
Secretions in expiratory valve housing; condensate in circuit; blood/sputum contamination on flow sensor; no decontamination certificate.
Harm
Bloodborne pathogen transmission (HBV, HCV, HIV); TB or drug-resistant organism contact; MRSA carriage transfer.
Control
Decontamination certificate before EBME handles device; universal precautions; PPE (gloves, apron, FFP3 for aerosol-generating procedures); hand hygiene.
Regulation
COSHH 2002 · MHRA DB2006(06)
📊
E.03
Post-Service Flow / Volume Inaccuracy (Patient Harm)
CRITICAL
Cause
Flow sensor replaced but not calibrated; compliance test not re-run after circuit change; expiratory valve reassembled incorrectly; leak test skipped.
Harm
Patient receives wrong tidal volume on return to service → barotrauma or hypoventilation depending on direction of error.
Control
Mandatory Vt accuracy test at ≥3 compliance settings post-service; leak test; compliance compensation verification; PPM checklist sign-off before return to use.
🫧
E.04
O₂ Sensor Mis-calibration
MAJOR
Cause
Galvanic fuel cell replaced but not calibrated to 21% (room air) and 100% O₂; wrong reference gas used; sensor connected in wrong orientation; expired cell installed.
Harm
FiO₂ under/over-reads → wrong O₂ delivery to patient. Low-reading → clinician increases O₂ → hyperoxia (retinopathy in neonates, oxygen toxicity in COPD). High-reading → hypoxia.
Control
Two-point calibration (21% + 100%) after every sensor replacement; traceable reference gas; certificate; cell expiry date check before use.
🌬️
E.05
Compressor / Turbine Contamination
MAJOR
Cause
Blocked inlet filters on compressor-driven ventilators; legionella/pseudomonas in humidifier water; dirty turbine housing after patient use; filter not replaced at PPM.
Harm
Pathogen delivery directly to patient's airway; compressor overheating and shutdown; reduced flow at high demand.
Control
Inlet filter inspection/replacement at PPM; sterile water in humidifier (no tap water); log compressor running hours; turbine bearing check; decontamination before service.
💨
E.06
High-Pressure O₂ Release Hazard
MAJOR
Cause
O₂ hose disconnection under pressure; pressure regulator failure; O₂ pipeline pressure spike during servicing; O₂ enriched atmosphere near ignition source.
Harm
O₂-enriched environment → rapid fire escalation; high-pressure hose whip injury; decompression burn from rapid O₂ release.
Control
Depressurise before disconnecting O₂ hoses; oil-free tools near O₂ connections; no grease on O₂ connectors; ventilate area when working with O₂ pipelines.
Regulation
HTM 02-01 · MHRA O₂ safety guidance
💾
E.07
Firmware Rollback / Software Error
MAJOR
Cause
Board replacement installs older firmware; interrupted update leaves partial firmware; alarm configuration reset to defaults; ventilation mode unavailable after update.
Harm
Missing alarm logic (e.g., disconnect alarm absent in old firmware); incorrect tidal volume calculation in old versions; mode unavailable at critical moment.
Control
Verify firmware version before and after service; OEM-approved update path only; document version in asset record; functional test all alarms after update.
Regulation
IEC 62304 software lifecycle · MHRA FSN process
🔬
E.08
Inadequate ESR Re-test After Repair
MAJOR
Cause
PSU or mains wiring repaired without IEC 62353 re-test; earth conductor not reconnected properly; heated humidifier circuit repaired without leakage re-test.
Harm
Undetected earth fault → leakage current rise → micro-shock via patient breathing circuit (CF classification violated → >10 µA).
Control
IEC 62353 after any mains-side repair: earth ≤0.1 Ω; enclosure leakage; CF patient leakage ≤10 µA. Log test results with approved analyser serial number.
Regulation
IEC 62353 · NHS HTM 06-01
§ 04 · Alarm System
IEC 60601-1-8
Alarm Classification
Ventilator alarm logic is more complex than infusion devices — pressure, volume, flow, gas, and ventilator-patient interaction all require independent alarm chains. Mandated alarms cannot be disabled.
HIGH PRIORITY
Immediate Action
· Red flashing visual
· 10-pulse repeating burst
· ≥65 dBA at 1 m
· Max 2 min silence period
· Cannot be disabled
MEDIUM PRIORITY
Prompt Response
· Yellow flashing visual
· 3-pulse repeating burst
· ≥45 dBA at 1 m
· Max 15 min silence
· Can be paused briefly
LOW PRIORITY
Awareness
· Cyan / steady visual
· Single chime or none
· Audio optional
· No silence limit
· Advisory only

§ 05 · Engineered Controls
Built-In Safety
Mechanisms
These are the design-embedded defences that act before any human response is required. Their integrity must be verified at every PPM.
01
💥
Mechanical · Patient
Pressure Relief Valve (Pop-Off)
Mechanical spring valve opens automatically if airway pressure exceeds a preset maximum (typically 60–80 cmH₂O). Acts even if software alarms fail — purely passive defence against barotrauma.
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.12.4.2 — mandatory
02
🧠
Electronic · Safety Architecture
Dual-CPU Watchdog Safety System
Independent safety processor monitors main CPU via hardware watchdog. On main CPU failure, safety CPU halts inspiration, triggers alarm, and can switch to safe backup mode — fully independent of application firmware.
IEC 62304 Class C · IEC 61508 SIL-2
03
🎛️
Software · Patient
Mandatory High/Low Pressure Alarms
Per ISO 80601-2-12, disconnect (low pressure) and high pressure alarms are MANDATORY and CANNOT be disabled. They must have independent signal paths from the main ventilation algorithm.
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.12 — cannot disable
04
🔋
Electrical · Continuity
Internal Battery with BMS
Li-ion or sealed lead-acid battery provides minimum 2–4 hr autonomy (transport: 6+ hr). BMS prevents overcharge, deep discharge, and thermal runaway. Maintains ventilation through power failures and transport.
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.6.8.3 · IEC 62133
05
🫧
Electronic · Patient
Apnoea Backup Ventilation
If spontaneous breathing ceases (PSV/CPAP mode), ventilator automatically switches to a preset backup rate and Vt. Protects patients who tire and lose respiratory drive without nurse detection.
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.12.4.4
06
🌬️
Software · Patient
O₂ / Air Blender Limit Lock
Electronic gas blending enforces FiO₂ range 21–100%. On O₂ supply failure, switches to 100% air. On air failure, switches to 100% O₂. Prevents delivery of hypoxic mixtures regardless of user input.
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.12.6
07
⚡
Electrical · Patient
CF Applied Part Isolation
Patient breathing circuit connected parts are electrically isolated from mains via optocouplers/isolation transformers. Leakage current ≤10 µA normal, ≤50 µA single fault. Protects against micro-shock via heated wire circuits.
IEC 60601-1 Table 1 — CF classification
08
🔔
Software · User
Three-Priority Alarm Framework
IEC 60601-1-8 compliant: High (red, ≥65 dBA), Medium (amber), Low (cyan). Alarm signal path is independent from ventilation CPU. Alarm audio cannot be permanently silenced on High alarms.
IEC 60601-1-8 — mandated signal characteristics
09
🛡️
Software · Patient
Compliance Compensation
Measures circuit compliance (typically 1–3 mL/cmH₂O) and automatically corrects tidal volume delivery to account for volume lost in circuit compression. Ensures patient receives the prescribed Vt, not the set Vt minus circuit loss.
ISO 80601-2-12 accuracy requirements
10
🌡️
Mechanical · Patient
Expiratory Valve (PEEP / Anti-Asphyxia)
Electromechanically controlled expiratory valve maintains PEEP and prevents complete circuit closure. Anti-asphyxia mechanism ensures minimum expiratory flow path even on valve actuator failure — patient can breathe spontaneously.
ISO 80601-2-12 §201.11 — anti-asphyxia
11
🔐
Software · User
Key Lock / Tamper Prevention
PIN or physical key-lock prevents accidental or patient-initiated parameter changes. Protects critical settings during transport. Audits changes via event log.
IEC 62366 usability · site policy
12
📒
Software · Forensic
Event Log / Audit Trail
Non-volatile log of alarm events, parameter changes, mode switches, and fault codes. Critical for post-incident investigation. EBME should download at PPM and after every reported incident.
IEC 62304 · MHRA incident investigation
§ 06 · Swiss Cheese Model
Defence-in-Depth
Each layer has holes. Harm reaches the patient only when holes align across all layers simultaneously — the foundation of ISO 14971 risk-based design and MHRA incident investigation.
I
Inherent Design Controls
Hazard eliminated at the engineering design stage. Mechanical pressure relief valve acts independently of all software. Anti-asphyxia valve ensures minimum expiratory path on actuator failure. O₂ blender limits FiO₂ ≥21%. CF isolation removes micro-shock pathway. ISO 14971 risk analysis drives all design constraints.
Pop-off valve Anti-asphyxia valve CF isolation Blender FiO₂ floor Dual-CPU watchdog
II
Automatic Protection Devices
Sensors and control logic detect hazardous states and act without human involvement. Pressure sensor halts inspiration on high Ppeak. Apnoea detection activates backup ventilation. O₂ sensor triggers supply switchover. Watchdog halts ventilation on CPU fault.
Ppeak auto-halt Apnoea backup O₂ switchover Compliance correction Battery switchover
III
Alarms & Warnings
IEC 60601-1-8 three-priority alarm framework requires clinician attention. Critically, this layer FAILS when alarm fatigue causes silencing or ignoring of alarms. Mandatory alarms (disconnect, high pressure) cannot be disabled by design.
High pressure alarm Disconnect alarm Apnoea alarm FiO₂ alarm Battery alarm
IV
Clinical Procedures & Human Factors
Ventilator care bundles (VAP bundle), shift checklists, alarm limit verification protocol, independent double-check on parameter changes, circuit change procedures. IEC 62366 usability engineering makes interfaces resistant to use errors. Transport protocol ensures battery and O₂ sufficiency before any move.
VAP bundle Shift checklist Transport protocol Alarm limit verification IEC 62366 usability
V
Planned Preventive Maintenance (EBME)
Annual PPM verifies all safety mechanisms remain functional. Flow accuracy at multiple compliance settings, leak test, O₂ sensor calibration, pressure sensor calibration, alarm verification (audibility ≥65 dBA), battery capacity, expiratory valve inspection, and IEC 62353 electrical safety re-test. PPM is the EBME engineer's primary patient safety contribution.
Vt accuracy test Leak test O₂ sensor cal IEC 62353 ESR Battery capacity Expiratory valve
VI
Post-Market Surveillance & Vigilance
MHRA Yellow Card and MIR reporting, Field Safety Corrective Actions (FSCA), incident investigation, trend analysis across asset fleet. EU MDR 2017/745 / UK MDR 2002 mandate PMS, PSUR, and PMCF. ECRI/AAMI hazard alerts disseminated through EBME to clinical teams.
MHRA Yellow Card Datix incident FSN response PSUR / PMCF ECRI alerts
