§ 00 · Stakeholder Risk Map
The Anaesthesia Machine
Risk Landscape
An anaesthesia machine simultaneously controls oxygenation, ventilation, and consciousness. Its failure modes uniquely combine hypoxic risk, awareness risk, and occupational gas-exposure risk in one device.
Device Scope
Theatre Anaesthesia Workstation
Draeger Perseus/Primus · GE Aisys · Getinge Flow
Full workstation: gas blending, vaporizer(s), circle circuit, CO₂ absorber, integrated ventilator, agent/gas monitoring, scavenging.
Field / Draw-Over Anaesthesia
Triservice apparatus · Austere environments
Simplified, minimal-power systems used in resource-limited or military settings, relying on patient's own respiratory effort to draw gas through the vaporizer.
MRI-Compatible Anaesthesia System
Non-ferromagnetic construction
Adapted for use within or near the MRI suite, with materials and monitoring compatible with the magnetic field environment.

§ 01 · Patient Hazards
Direct Physiological Risk
A patient under general anaesthesia cannot compensate for or report a developing problem — every safeguard here substitutes for a patient who cannot say "something's wrong."
🫧
P.01
Hypoxic Gas Mixture Delivery
CRITICAL
Cause
O₂ pipeline failure with N₂O/air still flowing; proportioning (hypoxic guard) linkage worn or bypassed; flowmeter/rotameter tube crack causing false reading.
Harm
Hypoxic brain injury within minutes — one of the most catastrophic and preventable anaesthesia hazards on record.
Control
Mechanical hypoxic guard enforcing minimum ~21–25% O₂ regardless of N₂O flow setting; O₂ failure cut-off (Bosch/Ritchie whistle); independent FiO₂/O₂ analyser in the circuit.
Standard
ISO 80601-2-13 §201.12.1.101 — hypoxic guard
😳
P.02
Awareness Under Anaesthesia
CRITICAL
Cause
Vaporizer empty/undetected running low; agent delivery interrupted by a leak; TIVA pump occlusion without volatile backup; agent monitor not in use or miscalibrated.
Harm
Intraoperative awareness with recall — a recognised cause of severe, long-term psychological harm (PTSD) to the patient.
Control
Continuous end-tidal agent monitoring with low-MAC alarm; vaporizer fill-level visible gauge; BIS/depth-of-anaesthesia monitoring where indicated.
Standard
ISO 80601-2-13 §201.12.1 — agent monitoring
🔌
P.03
Breathing Circuit Disconnection / Leak
CRITICAL
Cause
Circuit connector not fully seated; cracked corrugated tubing; APL valve left open; bag/bellows leak from a perished seal.
Harm
Loss of ventilation and/or anaesthetic gas delivery under paralysis — the patient cannot breathe for themselves and cannot signal distress.
Control
Mandatory low airway pressure/disconnect alarm (cannot be disabled); pre-use leak test (WHO/AAGBI checklist); capnography confirms ongoing ventilation breath-by-breath.
🌬️
P.04
CO₂ Absorbent Exhaustion — Hypercapnia
MAJOR
Cause
Soda lime/absorbent not changed at the recommended interval or colour-change indicator not checked; channelling in the canister reducing effective absorption.
Harm
Rebreathing of CO₂ → hypercapnia → tachycardia, arrhythmia, and (in severe cases) cardiovascular collapse.
Control
Capnography with rising inspired CO₂ alarm (earliest and most reliable indicator); absorbent colour-change indicator dye; scheduled replacement protocol.
💊
P.05
Vaporizer Overdose
CRITICAL
Cause
Vaporizer tipped, allowing liquid agent into the bypass chamber; wrong agent filled (e.g., desflurane into a sevoflurane vaporizer); internal calibration drift.
Harm
Sudden high-concentration agent delivery causes profound hypotension, bradycardia, or cardiovascular collapse.
Control
Agent-specific keyed filling ports (Pin Index/agent-specific filler system prevents wrong-agent filling); end-tidal agent monitoring with high-MAC alarm; interlock preventing two vaporizers running simultaneously.
Standard
ISO 5360 — anaesthetic vaporizer requirements
🔥
P.06
Compound A / Absorbent Fire Risk
MODERATE
Cause
Desiccated (dried-out) CO₂ absorbent reacting exothermically with sevoflurane or desflurane, producing Compound A or, in extreme cases, a canister fire.
Harm
Nephrotoxic potential from Compound A (sevoflurane); rare but documented absorbent canister fires causing burns and equipment damage.
Control
Turn off fresh gas flow when machine is not in use for extended periods; use low-desiccation absorbents; replace absorbent per schedule regardless of apparent colour change.
🌡️
P.07
Barotrauma from APL/Pressure Relief Fault
MAJOR
Cause
Adjustable pressure-limiting (APL) valve stuck closed; ventilator relief valve fault; fresh gas flow too high with valve closed during manual ventilation.
Harm
Circuit pressure rises uncontrolled → barotrauma, pneumothorax, particularly dangerous in paediatric anaesthesia with smaller lung volumes.
Control
High airway pressure alarm; independent mechanical pressure-relief valve; PPM verification of APL valve function across its full range.
🌡️
P.08
Malignant Hyperthermia Trigger Cross-Contamination
CRITICAL
Cause
Machine previously used with a volatile agent not adequately flushed/decontaminated before use on an MH-susceptible patient requiring a trigger-free anaesthetic.
Harm
Residual volatile agent traces can precipitate a malignant hyperthermia crisis in a susceptible patient — a life-threatening hypermetabolic emergency.
Control
Dedicated "MH-clean" machine or validated flushing protocol (fresh gas flow flush + absorbent/circuit change) per AAGBI guidance; activated charcoal filters where used.
Regulation
Association of Anaesthetists MH guidance
§ 02 · Clinical User Hazards
Use Error &
Human Factors
The pre-use machine check is the single most important human defence layer in anaesthesia — and the one most vulnerable to time pressure between cases.
✅
U.01
Pre-Use Machine Check Skipped or Rushed
CRITICAL
Cause
WHO/Association of Anaesthetists checklist skipped between cases under time pressure; assumption that "it was fine this morning" carries through the list.
Harm
Undetected leak, absorbent exhaustion, or gas supply fault carried directly into the next case with an anaesthetised patient.
Control
Mandatory documented checklist before every case (Association of Anaesthetists Checklist for Anaesthetic Equipment); electronic self-test integrated into modern workstations.
🧴
U.02
Wrong Agent Filled Into Vaporizer
CRITICAL
Cause
Bypassing the agent-specific keyed filler system; using a non-keyed funnel; mislabelled agent bottle.
Harm
Delivery of an unintended agent at an uncalibrated concentration — unpredictable depth of anaesthesia and cardiovascular effect.
Control
Agent-specific keyed filling systems must never be bypassed; two-person check on vaporizer filling; colour-coded agent labelling per ISO standard.
🔛
U.03
Vaporizer Left On After Case
MAJOR
Cause
Vaporizer dial not returned to zero at end of case; distraction during handover/turnover.
Harm
Unintended agent exposure to recovery staff or the next patient if fresh gas flow is started before the dial is checked.
Control
End-of-case checklist item; audible/visual indicator of non-zero vaporizer setting on modern workstations.
📏
U.04
Flowmeter / Fresh Gas Flow Misread
MAJOR
Cause
Traditional rotameter tube misread (ball position parallax); confusion between O₂ and N₂O/air flow controls under time pressure.
Harm
Inappropriate FiO₂ delivered; risk compounded on older machines without electronic gas mixture display.
Control
Electronic gas mixture display on modern workstations; standardised flow-control knob shapes/colours (O₂ always distinct); independent O₂ analyser cross-check.
💨
U.05
Occupational Waste Gas Exposure
MODERATE
Cause
Scavenging system disconnected, blocked, or not activated; poor mask seal during induction; high fresh gas flows without adequate extraction.
Harm
Chronic low-level volatile agent exposure to theatre staff — associated in the literature with headache, fatigue, and reproductive health concerns at sustained high exposure.
Control
Active scavenging system with flow verification; theatre air-change rate compliance (HTM 03-01); occupational exposure monitoring where indicated.
Regulation
COSHH 2002 · HTM 03-01
🎛️
U.06
APL Valve Mismanagement (Manual Ventilation)
MAJOR
Cause
APL valve left fully closed during bag-mask ventilation; valve position not adjusted when switching between spontaneous and controlled ventilation.
Harm
Excessive circuit pressure delivered to the patient — particularly hazardous in paediatric cases with a low pressure-injury threshold.
Control
Training on APL valve function and correct positioning for each ventilation mode; pressure gauge always visible during manual ventilation.
§ 03 · Service Engineer Hazards
Occupational &
Patient-Forward Risk
Anaesthesia machine servicing uniquely combines pressurised medical gas work with precision vaporizer calibration — errors in either propagate directly to an anaesthetised, non-consenting-in-the-moment patient.
⚠
Regulatory context Vaporizer and gas-delivery calibration errors are among the most serious reportable events in anaesthesia equipment servicing. Post-service agent-analyser verification is not optional.
📊
E.01
Vaporizer Calibration Error
CRITICAL
Cause
Vaporizer serviced/refilled and returned without agent-analyser verification across the dial range; wrong agent used during bench test; internal temperature-compensation bellows fault.
Harm
Machine delivers an incorrect anaesthetic concentration on its next clinical use — directly causes either awareness (under-delivery) or cardiovascular collapse (over-delivery).
Control
Mandatory calibrated gas analyser (Riken/agent-specific) verification across the full dial range before return to service; documented with analyser serial number and traceability certificate.
Standard
ISO 5360 · manufacturer service manual
🔀
E.02
Pipeline Gas Crossover After Install/Repair
CRITICAL
Cause
Non-interchangeable (NIST/Schrader) gas fittings bypassed or incorrectly reassembled after pipeline or machine-side work; hose assembly error during installation.
Harm
Wrong gas delivered to the patient port (e.g., N₂O where O₂ is expected) — a catastrophic, well-documented category of anaesthesia incident.
Control
Mandatory gas-specific crossover test after any pipeline or machine gas-supply work, per HTM 02-01; never defeat or adapt non-interchangeable fittings.
Regulation
HTM 02-01 · MHRA gas safety guidance
🛡️
E.03
Hypoxic Guard Bypassed During Test
CRITICAL
Cause
Proportioning linkage disconnected for testing and not correctly reassembled/re-verified; test performed with the O₂ failure alarm disabled and not re-enabled.
Harm
Machine returned to service with its principal defence against hypoxic gas delivery silently disabled.
Control
Mandatory hypoxic guard function test (progressively reduce O₂ flow and confirm N₂O cuts out at the correct ratio) before every return to service; checklist sign-off.
🩸
E.04
Biological Exposure — Breathing Circuit
MAJOR
Cause
Circuit, absorber, or bellows returned with secretions/blood contamination; no decontamination certificate accompanying the returned machine.
Harm
Bloodborne pathogen or respiratory pathogen exposure to the engineer during disassembly.
Control
Decontamination certificate required before EBME accepts the machine; universal precautions; PPE for circuit and absorber handling.
Regulation
COSHH 2002 · MHRA DB2006(06)
💨
E.05
High-Pressure Gas Release Hazard
MAJOR
Cause
Pipeline or cylinder hose disconnected under pressure; regulator failure; work performed on a pressurised line without depressurising first.
Harm
High-pressure hose whip injury; rapid decompression burns; O₂-enriched atmosphere near an ignition source.
Control
Depressurise before disconnecting; oil-free tools near O₂ connections; no grease on O₂ fittings; ventilate the work area.
Regulation
HTM 02-01
🧪
E.06
CO₂ Absorbent Canister Reseal Incomplete
MODERATE
Cause
Absorber canister disassembled for inspection/replacement and reseated without a proper gasket seal; O-ring degraded and not replaced.
Harm
Circuit leak reducing effective CO₂ scrubbing and fresh gas delivery — may not be caught by a routine leak test if the leak is small.
Control
Full circuit leak test after any absorber work; visual gasket/O-ring inspection at every PPM; replace seals per OEM service interval.

§ 05 · Engineered Controls
Built-In Safety
Mechanisms
These are the design-embedded defences that act before any human response is required — several are purely mechanical, by deliberate design choice, so they work even during a total power loss.
01
🛡️
Mechanical · Patient
Hypoxic Guard (Proportioning Linkage)
A purely mechanical linkage between the O₂ and N₂O flow controls physically prevents the O₂ fraction from falling below a safe minimum, regardless of electronic failure.
ISO 80601-2-13 §201.12.1.101
02
🔊
Mechanical/Pneumatic · Patient
O₂ Failure Alarm (Bosch/Ritchie Whistle)
A pneumatically powered whistle sounds on loss of O₂ pipeline pressure, independent of the machine's electrical supply — works even during total power failure.
ISO 80601-2-13 — independent of electrical power
03
🧴
Mechanical · Patient
Agent-Specific Keyed Filling System
Vaporizer filling ports are keyed to a specific agent, physically preventing the wrong volatile agent from being introduced.
ISO 5360 — vaporizer filling systems
04
🔒
Mechanical · Patient
Vaporizer Interlock
Prevents more than one vaporizer being engaged into the fresh gas pathway at the same time, eliminating unintended dual-agent delivery.
ISO 80601-2-13 — interlock requirement
05
🔌
Mechanical · Patient
Non-Interchangeable Gas Fittings (NIST/Schrader)
Gas-specific connector geometry physically prevents cross-connection between O₂, N₂O, air, and vacuum lines at the pipeline and machine level.
ISO 5359 — pipeline hose assemblies
06
📈
Electronic · Patient
Continuous Agent & Capnography Monitoring
End-tidal agent concentration and CO₂ are monitored breath-by-breath, providing the earliest and most reliable warning of awareness risk or absorbent exhaustion.
ISO 80601-2-55 — respiratory gas monitors
07
🎚️
Mechanical · Patient
Adjustable Pressure-Limiting (APL) / Relief Valve
Provides a manually or automatically controlled pressure ceiling in the breathing circuit, backed by an independent mechanical relief valve for absolute overpressure protection.
ISO 80601-2-13 §201.11
08
🌬️
Mechanical · Occupational
Active Scavenging System
Continuously extracts waste anaesthetic gas from the circuit and theatre environment, protecting staff from chronic low-level exposure.
HTM 03-01 · COSHH 2002
09
🧪
Chemical · Patient
CO₂ Absorbent Colour-Change Indicator
Dye additive in the soda lime changes colour as absorptive capacity is exhausted, giving a simple visual cue for timely replacement.
Manufacturer product specification
§ 06 · Swiss Cheese Model
Defence-in-Depth
Each layer has holes. Harm reaches the patient only when holes align across all layers simultaneously.
I
Inherent Design Controls
The hypoxic guard, non-interchangeable gas fittings, and agent-specific keyed vaporizer fillers all remove hazards mechanically, at source, independent of any electronic system.
Hypoxic guardNIST/Schrader fittingsKeyed vaporizer fillerVaporizer interlock
II
Automatic Protection Devices
O₂ failure whistle sounds without electrical power. Mechanical pressure-relief valve caps circuit pressure independent of software.
O₂ failure whistleMechanical relief valveAbsorbent colour indicator
III
Alarms & Warnings
Continuous agent monitoring, capnography, and circuit pressure alarms all require the anaesthetist's attention at the correct moment.
Agent concentration alarmCO₂ alarmCircuit pressure alarm
IV
Clinical Procedures & Human Factors
WHO/Association of Anaesthetists pre-use checklist, two-person vaporizer filling check, end-of-case dial-zero verification, MH-clean machine protocol.
Pre-use checklistTwo-person fill checkEnd-of-case checkMH-clean protocol
V
Planned Preventive Maintenance (EBME)
Annual hypoxic guard function test, agent-analyser calibration verification across the dial range, gas crossover test after any pipeline work, circuit leak test, scavenging flow verification.
Hypoxic guard testAgent analyser cal.Crossover testLeak testScavenging test
VI
Post-Market Surveillance & Vigilance
MHRA Yellow Card and MIR reporting, FSCA response, incident investigation for any awareness or hypoxia event, ECRI/AAMI hazard alert dissemination.
