The General Electrical
Risk Landscape
Electrical hazards cut across every device an EBME engineer touches. Unlike device-specific risks, the same handful of principles — leakage current, earth continuity, applied part classification — govern almost every incident.
Scope

Type B · BF · CF

Medical IT Power Systems

Portable Appliance & Recurrent Testing

§ 01 · Patient Hazards
Direct Physiological Risk
Electrical harm to a patient is almost always the failure of an invisible barrier — insulation, isolation, or earthing — rather than an obvious mechanical fault.
⚡
P.01
Macroshock — Earth Fault / Exposed Conductive Parts
CRITICAL
Cause
Protective earth conductor broken or corroded; insulation breakdown allowing mains contact with an accessible metal part; patient in contact with two devices at different potentials.
Harm
Current through skin contact (not requiring a direct cardiac path) — burns, muscle tetany, cardiac arrhythmia at higher currents.
Control
Earth continuity ≤0.1 Ω (Class I equipment); enclosure leakage current limits; RCD protection on the supply circuit; IEC 62353 recurrent testing.
Standard
IEC 60601-1 §8 · IEC 62353
💓
P.02
Microshock — CF Applied Part Fault
CRITICAL
Cause
Isolation barrier degraded in a device with a direct or near-direct intracardiac connection (IBP, CVP, pacing leads); leakage current as low as 10 µA reaching the heart directly.
Harm
Ventricular fibrillation at currents far below the threshold for macroshock — because the current bypasses skin resistance entirely via an intracardiac path.
Control
CF classification: ≤10 µA leakage normal condition, ≤50 µA single fault; double isolation barrier design; annual IEC 62353 patient leakage verification.
Standard
IEC 60601-1 Table 1 — CF classification
🔥
P.03
Diathermy Return Electrode Burn
CRITICAL
Cause
Patient return electrode (neutral plate) poorly applied, partially detached, or applied over a bony prominence/scar tissue with poor conductivity; return electrode monitoring (REM) circuit fault.
Harm
High-frequency surgical current concentrates at the point of poor contact, causing a severe burn at a site distant from the surgical field.
Control
Return electrode monitoring (REM) circuit that halts diathermy output if electrode contact impedance rises above a safe threshold; annual diathermy analyzer verification.
Standard
IEC 60601-2-2 — HF surgical equipment
🏷️
P.04
Wrong Applied Part Classification in Use
MAJOR
Cause
A Type BF-rated accessory (e.g., an SpO₂ probe) used in a role that places it in an effective intracardiac position, exceeding its designed isolation level.
Harm
Leakage current within the BF spec (≤100 µA) but exceeding the safe CF threshold for a direct cardiac path — a classification mismatch rather than a component fault.
Control
Understand and respect the applied part classification of every accessory before clinical deployment; never substitute accessories across classification boundaries.
➕
P.05
Leakage Current Summation — Multiple Devices
MAJOR
Cause
Several devices simultaneously connected to the same patient (monitor, infusion pump, ventilator), each within its own individual leakage spec, but combining at the patient connection point.
Harm
Cumulative leakage current at the patient exceeds a safe threshold even though no single device is individually faulty.
Control
System-level leakage assessment for any multi-device patient configuration (particularly in critical care); IEC 60601-1 total patient leakage current considerations.
🔥
P.06
Static Discharge in Oxygen-Enriched Environment
MODERATE
Cause
Electrostatic discharge (e.g., from synthetic bedding, movement) occurring near a free-flowing oxygen source close to the patient's face/chest.
Harm
A spark in an oxygen-enriched atmosphere can ignite a flash fire — a documented incident category, notably during defibrillation but relevant to any spark source near free-flowing O₂.
Control
Anti-static flooring and materials in high-O₂ areas per HTM guidance; direct O₂ away from the patient's chest/face where practical during any procedure with a spark risk.
§ 02 · Clinical User Hazards
Use Error &
Human Factors
Most clinical-user electrical hazards are entirely preventable through basic visual inspection and adherence to the testing schedule — the barrier here is workload, not knowledge.
🔌
U.01
Damaged Mains Lead / Plug Still in Use
MAJOR
Cause
Visible cable damage, exposed conductors, or a cracked plug not reported or removed from service; device continues to be used because it "still works."
Harm
Direct shock risk to whoever handles the lead — patient, visitor, or staff member — and fire risk from an arcing connection.
Control
"See something, remove something" culture: any visible damage takes the device out of service immediately pending EBME inspection; visual check as part of routine use.
📋
U.02
PAT / IEC 62353 Test Overdue
MAJOR
Cause
Asset test label expired and not checked before use; device brought back into service after storage without re-test; equipment missed during a PPM round.
Harm
A device in active clinical use may be carrying an undetected earth or insulation fault, with no engineering verification in place.
Control
Clear test-due labelling visible to ward staff; device availability audits; simple "check the label" habit before use.
🔀
U.03
Multi-Way Adaptors in Clinical Areas
MAJOR
Cause
Insufficient wall sockets leading to unofficial multi-way adaptors or extension leads being used to power multiple pieces of medical equipment.
Harm
Circuit overload risk; defeats the earthing and current-limiting design assumptions of the fixed installation; fire risk.
Control
Trust policy prohibiting non-medical-grade adaptors in clinical areas; escalate socket-provision shortfalls to Estates rather than working around them.
Regulation
NHS HTM 06-01
💧
U.04
Wet-Environment Electrical Hazard
MAJOR
Cause
Electrical equipment used or stored near sinks, bathing areas, or in theatre with fluid spillage risk; splash guards missing or bypassed.
Harm
Water dramatically reduces skin/contact resistance, increasing the severity of any shock from an otherwise minor fault.
Control
IP-rated equipment enclosures appropriate to the location; RCD protection in wet areas per BS 7671 special locations requirements.
👁️
U.05
Visible Damage Ignored
MAJOR
Cause
Cracked casing, scorch marks, or unusual smell/noise from a device not reported due to time pressure or assumption that it's "probably fine."
Harm
Early warning signs of an internal fault are missed, allowing a minor issue to progress to a shock or fire hazard.
Control
Simple, low-friction fault-reporting process; positive safety culture where reporting is never treated as a nuisance.
🔋
U.06
Non-Medical-Grade Equipment at the Bedside
MODERATE
Cause
Personal fans, phone chargers, or consumer electronics brought in by patients/visitors and used within reach of the patient without electrical safety verification.
Harm
Non-medical-grade leakage current limits and insulation standards do not meet the requirements for equipment in the patient environment.
Control
Trust policy on personal electrical items in clinical areas; PAT testing of any approved personal item before use near a patient.
§ 03 · Service Engineer Hazards
Occupational &
Patient-Forward Risk
Electrical work is the one category of EBME activity where the engineer's own safety and the patient's future safety are both directly at stake in the same task.
⚠
Foundational principle Every electrical hazard in this reference — across every device type in this site — traces back to one of three failures: a broken earth path, a degraded isolation barrier, or an untested/miscalibrated safety mechanism. Mastering general electrical safety is the foundation for every device-specific page.
⚡
E.01
Electric Shock — Live Working
CRITICAL
Cause
Fault-finding on an energised PCB; assuming a device is isolated without verifying; damaged or incorrectly rated test leads.
Harm
230 V AC mains contact — VF risk, flash burns, muscle tetany preventing self-release.
Control
Lock-out/tag-out (LOTO); isolation transformer for any necessary live diagnostics; Cat-rated test leads per IEC 61010; prove-dead procedure before touching any conductor.
Regulation
Electricity at Work Regs 1989 · IEC 61010-1
💥
E.02
Arc Flash
CRITICAL
Cause
Short circuit created while working on or near an energised high-current circuit; damaged test lead insulation; incorrect meter setting for the circuit under test.
Harm
Severe burns from thermal radiation and molten metal spray; potential permanent injury even without direct electrical contact.
Control
Correct CAT-rating for the circuit being tested; appropriate PPE for higher-energy work; isolate before working wherever feasible rather than relying on protective equipment alone.
🔋
E.03
Residual Capacitor Charge
CRITICAL
Cause
Power supply or HV storage capacitor (defibrillators, diathermy units, X-ray generators) retains a charge after mains and battery removal; assumption that power-off equals discharged.
Harm
A stored charge can deliver a significant, potentially lethal shock well after the device has been switched off and unplugged.
Control
Verify discharge via service menu/test point or manufacturer-specified wait time before opening any case with known HV storage; never assume — confirm.
🧰
E.04
Incorrect or Uncalibrated Test Equipment
MAJOR
Cause
IEC 62353 analyser or multimeter used past its own calibration due date; wrong CAT rating for the circuit; test leads damaged but still in use.
Harm
A false-pass result on a genuinely faulty device — the device is returned to clinical use believing it to be electrically safe when it is not.
Control
Test equipment on its own calibration schedule, traceable to a national standard; pre-use function check (test on a known-good reference) before each testing session.
🧑
E.05
Lone Working on Electrical Systems
MAJOR
Cause
Higher-risk electrical fault-finding or HV work performed without a second person present or a check-in protocol.
Harm
If an incident occurs, no one is present to isolate the supply, provide first aid, or raise the alarm promptly.
Control
Two-person rule for defined higher-risk tasks (live mains probing, HV capacitor work); lone-worker check-in system for all other electrical service work.
🔌
E.06
Isolation Transformer Misuse
MAJOR
Cause
Isolation transformer used beyond its rated capacity; earth reference incorrectly assumed on the isolated side; multiple devices daisy-chained off a single isolation transformer.
Harm
The protective benefit of isolation is defeated — a second fault can complete a shock circuit that the transformer was meant to prevent.
Control
One device per isolation transformer at a time; verify rated capacity before use; training on correct isolated-supply working principles.
§ 04 · Detection & Test
Fault Detection &
Recurrent Test Regime
Electrical safety has no continuous "alarm" in the way a ventilator or monitor does — detection instead relies on a recurring cycle of scheduled tests and passive protective devices.
FAIL CRITERIA
Immediate Action
· Earth continuity >0.1 Ω
· CF leakage >10 µA
· Insulation resistance below limit
· Device removed from service immediately
MARGINAL RESULT
Investigate Before Return
· Result close to limit but passing
· Trend against previous test results
· Re-test after any environmental change
PASS
Routine Recurrence
· Within IEC 62353 limits
· Documented with analyser serial number
· Next test date recorded on asset

§ 05 · Engineered Controls
Built-In Safety
Mechanisms
These principles recur across every device category in this reference — understanding them here makes every device-specific hazard page easier to reason about.
01
🏷️
Design Classification · Patient
Applied Part Classification (B / BF / CF)
Every patient-contacting part is classified by its isolation level and permitted leakage current, matched to the clinical risk of its use — CF for any part with a direct or effective intracardiac path.
IEC 60601-1 Table 1
02
🛡️
Construction · Patient
Class I / Class II Equipment Construction
Class I relies on protective earthing plus basic insulation; Class II uses double or reinforced insulation with no reliance on an earth connection at all.
IEC 60601-1 §8.4 — protection classes
03
🔌
Installation · Occupational & Patient
Medical IT (Isolated Power) Systems
In theatres and critical care, an isolated power supply with a line isolation monitor allows a first earth fault to be flagged and investigated without automatic disconnection mid-procedure.
NHS HTM 06-01 — medical IT systems
04
🔴
Fixed Installation · Occupational
Residual Current Devices (RCDs)
Detect an earth-leakage imbalance and disconnect the circuit automatically, protecting against both macroshock to people and downstream equipment faults.
BS 7671 — wiring regulations
05
🔥
Electronic · Patient
Return Electrode Monitoring (REM)
Continuously monitors contact impedance of the diathermy return electrode, halting output before a poor-contact burn can occur.
IEC 60601-2-2 — HF surgical equipment
06
🔻
Electrical · Engineer
Isolation Transformers for Live Diagnostics
Floats the equipment under test relative to earth, so a single fault while probing does not complete a shock circuit through the engineer.
One device per transformer — do not daisy-chain
07
🔻
Electrical · Engineer & Patient
Automatic HV Discharge Circuits
Dump resistors and bleed circuits safely discharge stored energy in HV devices (defibrillators, diathermy, X-ray generators) — the design pattern that keeps residual-charge risk manageable.
Verify at every PPM on HV-storage devices
08
📒
Process · Forensic
IEC 62353 Recurrent Test Regime
Scheduled, documented electrical safety testing at defined intervals — and always after any repair — forms the backbone of ongoing electrical safety assurance for every device in the fleet.
IEC 62353 — recurrent test and test after repair
09
🔒
Process · Occupational
Lock-Out / Tag-Out (LOTO)
Physical isolation and labelling of a circuit under work, preventing re-energisation by another person while the engineer is still exposed.
Electricity at Work Regs 1989
§ 06 · Swiss Cheese Model
Defence-in-Depth
Each layer has holes. Electrical harm — to patient or engineer — occurs only when holes align across every layer simultaneously.
I
Inherent Design Controls
Applied part classification (B/BF/CF) and Class I/II construction remove hazards at the design stage, matched to the intended clinical use of each device and accessory.
Applied part classificationClass I/II constructionDouble isolation barriers
II
Automatic Protection Devices
RCDs disconnect on earth-leakage imbalance. Return electrode monitoring halts diathermy on poor contact. Line isolation monitors flag a first fault in theatre without disconnecting.
RCD protectionREM cutoffLine isolation monitor
III
Test-Cycle Detection
Unlike continuous alarms, electrical safety relies on a recurring test cycle — IEC 62353 earth continuity, insulation resistance, and leakage current checks at scheduled intervals and after repair.
Test-due labellingIEC 62353 scheduleTest-after-repair rule
IV
Clinical & Occupational Procedures
"Remove on visible damage" culture, prohibition of unofficial multi-way adaptors, wet-area equipment protocols, lone-worker rules for higher-risk electrical tasks.
Visible damage reportingAdaptor policyLone-worker protocol
V
EBME Verification Practice
LOTO before any internal work, isolation transformers for necessary live diagnostics, calibrated Cat-rated test equipment, verified HV discharge before opening any case with known energy storage.
LOTOIsolation transformer useCalibrated test equipmentHV discharge verification
VI
Post-Market Surveillance & Vigilance
MHRA Yellow Card and MIR reporting for any electrical incident, RIDDOR reporting of engineer injuries, fleet-wide trend analysis of test failures by manufacturer/model.
MHRA Yellow CardRIDDOR reportingFleet trend analysis
