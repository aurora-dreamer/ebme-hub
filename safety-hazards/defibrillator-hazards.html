§ 00 · Stakeholder Risk Map
The Defibrillator
Risk Landscape
A defibrillator stores and delivers hundreds of joules through a patient in milliseconds. The same stored energy that saves a life in cardiac arrest is capable of killing a bystander or engineer if released uncontrolled.
Device Scope
Manual Biphasic Defibrillator
Resus trolley · ICU · ED · Theatre
Clinician-selected energy, manual paddles or pads, sync cardioversion, pacing option. Requires trained rhythm interpretation.
AED (Automated External)
Ward · Public access · Community
Automated rhythm analysis and voice-prompted shock advisory. Designed for lay-rescuer and first-responder use.
Combined Monitor/Defibrillator
Critical care transport · Ambulance
Integrated ECG/SpO₂/NIBP monitoring with defibrillation and pacing in one device — used through the full peri-arrest pathway.

§ 01 · Patient Hazards
Direct Physiological Risk
Patient harm here is measured in seconds — either the wrong action delivered instantly, or the right action delayed. Both cost myocardium and brain tissue.
⏱️
P.01
Failed or Delayed Energy Delivery
CRITICAL
Cause
Capacitor fails to charge; battery depleted; pad impedance out of range blocking charge; charge timeout before shock delivered.
Harm
Every minute without defibrillation in VF reduces survival by ~7–10%. Delay directly costs survivable rhythm window.
Control
Daily/weekly automated self-test; battery capacity PPM; charge-time verification against spec; spare charged device on resus trolley.
Standard
IEC 60601-2-4 §201.12 — charge time limits
📉
P.02
Missed Shockable Rhythm (AED Mode)
CRITICAL
Cause
Rhythm classification algorithm misclassifies fine VF as asystole; CPR artefact corrupts ECG signal during analysis; outdated algorithm version.
Harm
No shock advised when one is needed — arrest progresses untreated. Reportable incident under MHRA vigilance.
Control
Validated against ANSI/AAMI DF80 rhythm library at manufacture; firmware version control; CPR artefact-filtering algorithms; do not modify analysis algorithm without OEM authorisation.
🔥
P.03
Skin Burns — Poor Contact / Arcing
MAJOR
Cause
Dried/expired pad gel; excess chest hair without shaving; pads placed over jewellery, ECG electrodes, or transdermal patches; paddle pressure too low.
Harm
Second-degree skin burns at contact points; energy loss to arcing reduces effective delivered dose to myocardium.
Control
Pad impedance gating blocks charge outside safe range; pre-use skin prep protocol; pad expiry stock control; remove transdermal patches/metal before placement.
💔
P.04
Sync Failure — Shock on Vulnerable Period
CRITICAL
Cause
Sync mode not selected for cardioversion; R-wave detection fails on low-amplitude or artefact-laden ECG; sync marker misaligned.
Harm
Shock delivered during the T-wave vulnerable period can induce VF — converting a stable rhythm into a cardiac arrest.
Control
Sync mode visibly indicated with marker on each QRS; device withholds shock if no valid R-wave detected; PPM verification of sync timing on simulator.
Standard
IEC 60601-2-4 §201.8.101 — synchronised mode
⚡
P.05
Energy Delivered Outside Tolerance
MAJOR
Cause
Aged/degraded HV capacitor; current-sense feedback fault; impedance compensation not functioning across patient range.
Harm
Under-delivery: ineffective defibrillation, arrest continues. Over-delivery: myocardial injury, increased post-shock arrhythmia risk.
Control
Annual defibrillator analyzer test across impedance range (25–200 Ω); waveform capture comparison; capacitor replacement per OEM service life.
🔥
P.06
Fire in Oxygen-Enriched Atmosphere
CRITICAL
Cause
Defibrillation attempted with free-flowing O₂ near the chest (nasal cannula, non-rebreathe mask left running) generating a spark at pad contact.
Harm
Flash fire directly on or near the patient's chest during resuscitation — a documented, entirely preventable cause of severe burns.
Control
Resuscitation Council/AHA guidance: remove or direct O₂ source away from chest before shock; use self-adhesive pads (fewer sparks than paddles + gel); staff training.
Regulation
Resuscitation Council UK guidance · HTM 02-01
🔋
P.07
Pacing Capture Failure (if fitted)
MAJOR
Cause
Pacing current set too low for transthoracic threshold; poor pad contact; underlying rhythm misread as captured.
Harm
Bradycardia/asystole untreated despite apparent pacing activity — a false sense of security for the treating team.
Control
Always confirm mechanical capture (palpable pulse), not just electrical pacing spikes; verify pacing output against test load at PPM.
🦠
P.08
Cross-Infection via Reused Paddles
MODERATE
Cause
Reusable paddles not decontaminated between patients; shared gel pot; paddle surface pitting harbouring biological material.
Harm
Bloodborne or skin pathogen transmission between patients — relevant given resus scenarios often involve open wounds or fluids.
Control
Single-use self-adhesive pads preferred; decontamination protocol for reusable paddles; inspect paddle face condition at PPM.
§ 02 · Clinical User Hazards
Use Error &
Human Factors
Defibrillation happens under extreme time pressure with a crowded resuscitation team. This is precisely the environment where checklists and interlocks matter most.
🙅
U.01
Shock Delivered Without "All Clear"
CRITICAL
Cause
Team member still in contact with patient/bed/trolley during shock; verbal "clear" call skipped under pressure; oxygen mask left touching chest.
Harm
Electric shock to rescuer — burns, cardiac arrhythmia in the responder themselves, particularly with wet skin/gloves.
Control
Mandatory visual scan and verbal "clear" before shock button; team training (ALS/ACLS); shock button requires deliberate press, not proximity-triggered.
🔀
U.02
Wrong Mode — Sync vs. Asynchronous
CRITICAL
Cause
Cardioversion attempted in default (async) mode; sync mode selected but not re-armed after each shock (many devices default back to async after delivery).
Harm
Unsynchronised shock on unstable tachycardia risks inducing VF; conversely, treating true VF in sync mode may delay shock waiting for a (non-existent) R-wave.
Control
Clear on-screen sync indicator; device design requiring active re-selection per shock; team training on mode selection for the specific arrhythmia.
📅
U.03
Expired Pads / Dried Gel
MAJOR
Cause
Pad expiry date not checked as part of daily trolley check; pads stored in hot/dry environment accelerating gel degradation.
Harm
High impedance blocks charge or reduces delivered energy at the exact moment it's needed — no time to source new pads mid-arrest.
Control
Daily resus trolley check includes pad expiry; stock rotation (first-expiry-first-out); spare pad set sealed with trolley.
🔴
U.04
Resus Trolley Device Not Ready
CRITICAL
Cause
Battery not on charge after last use; self-test failure not actioned; device borrowed from trolley and not returned; tamper seal broken but not checked.
Harm
Device unavailable or non-functional at the point of a cardiac arrest call — a completely preventable failure mode.
Control
Daily documented trolley/defibrillator check (self-test pass, battery status, tamper seal, pad expiry) per Resuscitation Council standards.
🔊
U.05
Voice Prompt Ignored or Misunderstood (AED)
MAJOR
Cause
Noisy environment masking voice prompts; language barrier for lay rescuer; panic overriding prompt-following behaviour.
Harm
Delayed or incorrect action during a public-access AED event — the entire safety model relies on prompt compliance.
Control
Combined voice + visual/text prompts; volume adequate for public spaces; regular public AED awareness training; simple, jargon-free prompt wording (IEC 62366 usability testing).
🔄
U.06
Delay from Unfamiliar Device Model
MAJOR
Cause
Multiple defibrillator models/manufacturers across a Trust with different button layouts, energy-select mechanisms, and pad connectors.
Harm
Seconds to minutes lost while a clinician searches for the charge button or energy selector on an unfamiliar unit — directly costs survival odds.
Control
Trust-wide standardisation on a single model where possible; mandatory induction training on all in-use models; consistent trolley layout across sites.
§ 03 · Service Engineer Hazards
Occupational &
Patient-Forward Risk
Defibrillators store lethal energy internally even when powered off. No other device in the EBME workshop carries this specific residual-energy risk profile.
⚠
Never assume discharged A defibrillator capacitor can retain a lethal charge after mains and battery removal. Always verify auto-discharge function and follow manufacturer lockout procedure before opening the case.
⚡
E.01
Residual HV Capacitor Shock
CRITICAL
Cause
Internal work performed without confirming capacitor discharge; dump-resistor circuit itself faulty; assumption that power-off equals safe.
Harm
Up to ~2000 V stored energy — capable of causing cardiac arrest in the engineer. This is the single highest-consequence hazard in defibrillator service.
Control
Verify auto-discharge via service menu/test point before opening case; manufacturer-specified wait time; insulated tools; never bridge HV terminals directly.
Regulation
Electricity at Work Regs 1989 · manufacturer service manual LOTO
📊
E.02
Post-Service Energy Delivery Inaccuracy
CRITICAL
Cause
Capacitor or H-bridge replaced without full defibrillator analyzer verification across the impedance range; charge-feedback divider not recalibrated.
Harm
Device returned to the resus trolley delivers inaccurate energy on its next real use — directly affects survival outcome in a future arrest.
Control
Mandatory energy accuracy test at low/mid/max settings across ≥3 impedances before return to service; documented in asset record with analyzer serial number.
🩸
E.03
Biological Exposure — Pads & Paddles
MAJOR
Cause
Device returned for repair still carrying blood, vomit, or gel residue from a resuscitation event; reusable paddles not decontaminated before handover to EBME.
Harm
Bloodborne pathogen exposure; contact with bodily fluids from a peri-arrest event, often without warning that the device was just used clinically.
Control
Decontamination certificate required before EBME accepts device; universal precautions; PPE; treat every post-use device as contaminated until proven otherwise.
💾
E.04
Firmware / Rhythm Algorithm Error
MAJOR
Cause
Firmware update interrupted or rolled back to an unvalidated version; rhythm analysis algorithm version mismatch with manufacturer's current DF80-validated release.
Harm
Shock advisory accuracy degraded on an AED unit — a subtle fault with no obvious symptom until a real arrest event.
Control
OEM-approved update path only; verify version before/after; functional rhythm-simulator test post-update; document in asset/firmware record.
Regulation
IEC 62304 · MHRA FSN process
🔍
E.05
Self-Test Failure Missed at PPM
MAJOR
Cause
Event log not reviewed at PPM; transient self-test failures dismissed without investigation; log cleared before root cause identified.
Harm
A device with an intermittent fault is returned to the resus trolley and may fail at the point of clinical need.
Control
Mandatory event log review before every PPM sign-off; investigate any repeated fault code even if the device currently "passes"; do not clear logs until root cause is resolved.
💥
E.06
Arc Flash from Analyzer Test Rig
MODERATE
Cause
Defibrillator analyzer test leads damaged or incorrectly connected; test performed at maximum energy with poor connection contact.
Harm
Arc flash burn to engineer; damage to test equipment; inaccurate reading leading to a false pass.
Control
Inspect analyzer leads before each use; secure connections before charge; maintain analyzer per its own calibration schedule.

§ 05 · Engineered Controls
Built-In Safety
Mechanisms
Each of these must be individually verified during PPM — a functioning "Charge Ready" tone tells you nothing about whether the dump resistor still works.
01
🔻
Electrical · Engineer & Patient
Automatic Dump-Resistor Discharge
Bleeds the HV capacitor safely if no shock is delivered within the timeout window or the energy setting is changed. The single most important safety mechanism for anyone handling the device internally.
Manufacturer service manual — verify every PPM
02
🧠
Electronic · Safety Architecture
Independent HV Enable Path
Shock trigger and HV enable circuit are gated separately from the main analysis CPU, so a single software fault cannot cause an unintended discharge.
IEC 60601-2-4 §201.15 — single-fault safe
03
🎯
Software · Patient
Pad Impedance Gating
Charge and shock are blocked outside the validated 25–200 Ω window, preventing ineffective delivery or excessive current on a low-impedance patient.
IEC 60601-2-4 — impedance compensation range
04
💓
Software · Clinical Safety
Sync Mode R-Wave Gate
In cardioversion mode, discharge is withheld until a valid R-wave is detected, avoiding delivery during the T-wave vulnerable period.
IEC 60601-2-4 §201.8.101
05
🎚️
Electronic · Patient
Impedance-Compensated Current Sensing
Real-time current feedback adjusts pulse duration and tilt during discharge, keeping delivered energy consistent across a wide range of patient impedances.
IEC 60601-2-4 — waveform accuracy
06
🔋
Electrical · Continuity
Automated Self-Test
Daily/weekly automated check of battery, capacitor charge/discharge path, and internal circuitry without full energy delivery. Result logged to event history.
Reviewed at PPM — do not dismiss transient failures
07
🔒
Mechanical · User
Deliberate Two-Action Shock Trigger
Manual mode requires a distinct charge action followed by a separate, deliberate shock-button press — reducing the chance of accidental discharge during handling.
IEC 62366 usability engineering
08
🩹
Electrical · Patient
Defib-Proof ECG Isolation
ECG front-end is isolated to survive the HV discharge pulse without damage and recover trace display within the standard-mandated window.
IEC 60601-2-27 — defib protection & recovery
09
📒
Software · Forensic
Event Log with Waveform Capture
Non-volatile log of every shock delivered, energy setting, impedance measured, and self-test result. Essential for post-incident review and PPM verification.
IEC 62304 · MHRA incident investigation
§ 06 · Swiss Cheese Model
Defence-in-Depth
Each layer has holes. Harm reaches the patient — or the engineer — only when holes align across all layers simultaneously.
I
Inherent Design Controls
Hazard eliminated at the engineering stage. Pad impedance gating prevents charge on poor contact. Sync gate prevents T-wave shock by design. Independent HV enable path prevents single-fault discharge.
Impedance gatingSync R-wave gateIndependent HV enableDefib-proof ECG isolation
II
Automatic Protection Devices
Dump-resistor auto-discharge bleeds the capacitor on timeout. Impedance-compensated current sensing keeps energy consistent. Automated self-test flags faults without human prompting.
Auto-dischargeCurrent-sense compensationAutomated self-test
III
Alarms & Prompts
Charge-ready tone, check-electrodes warning, sync-failure alert, and voice-prompted AED guidance all require operator attention at the correct moment.
Charge ready toneCheck electrodesAED voice promptsSync failure alert
IV
Clinical Procedures & Human Factors
ALS/ACLS training, verbal "all clear" protocol, daily resus trolley checks, standardisation on a single model per Trust where feasible.
Verbal "clear" protocolDaily trolley checkALS/ACLS trainingModel standardisation
V
Planned Preventive Maintenance (EBME)
Annual defibrillator analyzer test across the full energy and impedance range, dump-resistor verification, waveform capture comparison, sync timing test, event log review.
Energy accuracy testDump-resistor testWaveform captureSync timing testEvent log review
VI
Post-Market Surveillance & Vigilance
MHRA Yellow Card and MIR reporting, FSCA response, trend analysis across the resus-equipment fleet, ECRI/AAMI hazard alert dissemination.
