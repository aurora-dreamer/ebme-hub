§ 00 · Stakeholder Risk Map
The Patient Monitor
Risk Landscape
Patient monitors don't intervene — they watch. Their entire clinical value rests on trustworthy alarms; a monitor that silently fails is arguably more dangerous than no monitor at all.
Device Scope
ICU / Critical Care Monitor
Full parameter set · Central station linked
ECG, SpO₂, NIBP, IBP, temperature, CO₂. Continuous multi-parameter surveillance for the sickest patients on the unit.
Ward / Step-Down Monitor
Vital signs spot-check or continuous
Simplified parameter set (SpO₂, NIBP, HR), often mobile on a trolley, used for lower-acuity continuous or intermittent monitoring.
Transport Monitor
Battery-powered · Compact
Used during intra/inter-hospital transfer. Must maintain continuity of monitoring through handovers with no gap in surveillance.

§ 01 · Patient Hazards
Direct Physiological Risk
A monitor's failure mode is rarely dramatic — it is usually a quiet absence of warning while a patient deteriorates unobserved.
📉
P.01
Missed Deterioration — False Negative
CRITICAL
Cause
Alarm limits set too wide; sensor disconnected without triggering a technical alarm; arrhythmia algorithm fails to classify a genuine event.
Harm
Cardiac arrest, respiratory failure, or haemorrhage progresses unobserved until a clinician happens to check the patient.
Control
Mandatory alarm limits per protocol; validated arrhythmia algorithm (ANSI/AAMI EC13); technical "lead off"/"probe off" alarms distinct from physiological silence.
Standard
IEC 60601-2-27 §201.12 — HR alarm accuracy
🩹
P.02
NIBP Cuff — Nerve / Skin Injury
MAJOR
Cause
Over-frequent automatic cycling on a limb with poor perfusion; pump/valve fault causing prolonged inflation; wrong cuff size causing excessive local pressure.
Harm
Petechiae, bruising, compartment syndrome (rare but reported), transient nerve palsy from prolonged high cuff pressure.
Control
Independent hardware over-pressure relief valve separate from software limit; maximum cycle-frequency clinical protocol; cuff-site rotation for frequent monitoring.
Standard
IEC 80601-2-30 — overpressure protection
💉
P.03
IBP Zero Drift — Wrong Clinical Decision
CRITICAL
Cause
Transducer not re-zeroed after patient repositioning; drift in the amplifier/bridge circuit; reference level not at the phlebostatic axis.
Harm
Vasopressor titration or ICP management based on a biased pressure reading — a documented cause of serious harm in critical care.
Control
Mandatory zeroing at shift start and after any position change; PPM calibration against a reference manometer; visual confirmation of transducer height.
🖐️
P.04
SpO₂ Probe Pressure Injury
MODERATE
Cause
Probe left on the same digit for prolonged continuous monitoring, particularly in neonates or patients with poor peripheral perfusion.
Harm
Localised pressure necrosis or thermal injury from LED heating, especially in low-perfusion states where tissue tolerance is reduced.
Control
Probe site rotation protocol; correct probe size for patient (adult/paediatric/neonatal); skin inspection at routine intervals.
⚡
P.05
Electrical Micro-shock (CF Applied Part)
MAJOR
Cause
Leakage current via ECG or IBP lead to patient; insulation breakdown in a parameter module; fluid ingress at a connector.
Harm
VF at ≥10 µA via an intracardiac path (CF classification) — highest risk when an IBP or CVP line is also in place.
Control
CF applied part ≤10 µA leakage (normal); IEC 62353 annual re-test; isolation barrier verification on every parameter module.
Standard
IEC 60601-1 Table 1 · IEC 62353
🦠
P.06
Cross-Infection — Reusable Probes / Cuffs
MODERATE
Cause
Reusable SpO₂ probes and NIBP cuffs not decontaminated between patients per Trust protocol; damaged probe casing harbouring biological material.
Harm
Fomite transmission of pathogens including MRSA and C. difficile between successive patients.
Control
Decontamination protocol enforced between patients; single-patient-use disposables for high-risk/isolation patients; probe casing inspected at PPM.
🔌
P.07
IBP Line Disconnection — Exsanguination
CRITICAL
Cause
Arterial or central line disconnects from the transducer set; three-way tap left open; pressure trace flat-lines undetected.
Harm
Rapid, potentially life-threatening blood loss from an arterial line if disconnection goes unnoticed — a well-documented critical-care incident type.
Control
Mandatory IBP disconnect alarm (flat/zero trace); luer-lock connections; visible tubing runs; line checked at every handover.
🌡️
P.08
Temperature Probe Miscalibration
MODERATE
Cause
Thermistor drift outside spec; wrong probe type connected (skin vs. core) without software reconfiguration; poor probe contact.
Harm
Missed hypothermia/hyperthermia in vulnerable patients (neonates, post-op); incorrect core-peripheral gradient perfusion assessment.
Control
Annual verification against a precision resistance decade box or calibrated probe simulator at PPM.
§ 02 · Clinical User Hazards
Use Error &
Human Factors
Alarm fatigue is the single most studied hazard in patient monitoring, and it is fundamentally a human factors problem rather than a device fault.
🔔
U.01
Alarm Fatigue
CRITICAL
Cause
ICU/ward beds generate hundreds of alarms per day; the large majority are non-actionable (artefact, technical). Staff become desensitised and slower to respond.
Harm
A genuine critical alarm is missed among the noise — ECRI Institute has ranked alarm hazards among the top hospital technology hazards for over a decade.
Control
IEC 60601-1-8 three-priority framework; individualised, clinically appropriate alarm limits; smart/delayed alarm algorithms; EBME feedback loop on nuisance-alarm rate by ward.
Standard
IEC 60601-1-8 · AAMI/ECRI Alarm Safety guidance
📍
U.02
Wrong Lead / Probe Placement
MAJOR
Cause
ECG electrodes on incorrect anatomical positions; SpO₂ probe on a poorly perfused digit; IBP transducer at wrong height relative to the patient.
Harm
Misleading waveform morphology, inaccurate SpO₂ readings, or biased pressure values driving incorrect clinical decisions.
Control
Colour-coded electrode/lead labelling; standardised placement diagrams at the bedside; nurse/HCA training and competency sign-off.
🔇
U.03
Alarm Silenced and Not Restored
CRITICAL
Cause
Alarms paused for a procedure (repositioning, line change) and the pause is not manually cancelled or does not auto-resume as expected.
Harm
Monitor is effectively silent through a critical event, with the display suggesting normal surveillance is active.
Control
Time-limited pause with mandatory auto-resume (typically ≤2 min per Trust policy); visible on-screen countdown; cannot be indefinitely silenced by design.
🛏️
U.04
Wrong Patient / Bed Association at Central Station
MAJOR
Cause
Bed move not updated at the central monitoring station; monitor swapped between patients without re-labelling; network configuration error.
Harm
Alarm attributed to the wrong patient — remote monitoring staff respond to (or ignore) the wrong bed entirely.
Control
Mandatory bed/patient re-association check at every transfer; visual confirmation at central station; periodic audit of monitor-bed mapping.
📏
U.05
Incorrect NIBP Cuff Size
MODERATE
Cause
Cuff selected by convenience/availability rather than measured limb circumference; adult cuff used on a paediatric or bariatric patient.
Harm
The single most common cause of clinically significant NIBP inaccuracy — driving incorrect treatment of apparent hyper/hypotension.
Control
Cuff sizing chart at the bedside; multiple cuff sizes stocked per bay; training reinforcement during induction.
📡
U.06
Central Station Disconnect Unnoticed
MAJOR
Cause
Wireless/WMTS signal dropout between bedside monitor and central station; network outage; telemetry battery depleted.
Harm
Remote monitoring staff believe surveillance is continuing when the feed has silently dropped — a documented root cause in reported incidents.
Control
Central station "signal lost" technical alarm distinct from physiological alarms; network redundancy; telemetry battery-life protocol.
§ 03 · Service Engineer Hazards
Occupational &
Patient-Forward Risk
A monitor's failures are often invisible until the next clinical decision is made on a biased reading — post-service verification is where EBME carries most of the patient-forward risk.
⚠
Regulatory context Post-service verification of every parameter module affected by a repair is mandatory under UK MDR 2002. Failure to verify performance after repair is a potential MHRA reportable event if patient harm results.
⚡
E.01
Electric Shock — Mains Voltage
CRITICAL
Cause
Live PCB fault-finding on the main power supply; probing an energised module without isolation; damaged test leads.
Harm
230 V AC contact — VF risk, flash burns, muscle tetany preventing self-release.
Control
LOTO; isolation transformer for live diagnostics; Cat-rated test leads; competency sign-off; two-person rule for live mains probing.
Regulation
Electricity at Work Regs 1989 · IEC 61010-1
📊
E.02
Post-Service IBP / NIBP Miscalibration
CRITICAL
Cause
Parameter module replaced or repaired without re-verification against a calibrated pressure simulator/manometer; zero offset not confirmed.
Harm
Patient returns to a monitor delivering biased pressure readings — directly affects vasopressor titration and BP management decisions.
Control
Mandatory simulator verification across the clinical range post-service; documented in asset record with reference equipment serial number.
🩸
E.03
Biological Exposure — Cables & Probes
MAJOR
Cause
ECG leads, SpO₂ probes, and NIBP hoses returned for repair still carrying bodily fluids or secretions; no decontamination certificate.
Harm
Bloodborne pathogen exposure; contact with MRSA or other resistant organisms carried on returned accessories.
Control
Decontamination certificate required before EBME accepts the device; universal precautions; PPE for all cable/probe handling.
Regulation
COSHH 2002 · MHRA DB2006(06)
💾
E.04
Firmware Update Removes/Alters Alarm Logic
MAJOR
Cause
Firmware update resets alarm configuration to factory defaults; older firmware installed on a replacement board lacking current alarm logic.
Harm
A ward-specific mandated alarm setting is silently lost — patients monitored under an unintended default configuration.
Control
Verify firmware version and alarm configuration before/after any update; OEM-approved update path only; functional alarm test post-update.
Regulation
IEC 62304 · MHRA FSN process
☎️
E.05
Nurse-Call Relay Left Untested
MAJOR
Cause
Relay contact function not verified at PPM; ward call-system upgraded independently, breaking compatibility with the monitor's relay output.
Harm
A silent-failure point — the bedside alarm sounds correctly, but the corridor/nurse-station notification never fires.
Control
Mandatory nurse-call relay actuation test at every PPM; re-verify after any ward call-system change, even if the monitor itself wasn't serviced.
🔬
E.06
Inadequate Electrical Safety Re-test After Repair
MAJOR
Cause
Parameter module or PSU repaired without full IEC 62353 re-test; earth conductor not correctly reconnected on reassembly.
Harm
Undetected earth fault raises leakage current beyond CF classification via the ECG or IBP patient connection.
Control
IEC 62353 after any mains-side repair: earth ≤0.1 Ω; CF patient leakage ≤10 µA. Log test results with analyser serial number.
Regulation
IEC 62353 · NHS HTM 06-01

§ 05 · Engineered Controls
Built-In Safety
Mechanisms
These design-embedded defences must be individually verified at PPM — a monitor that "looks fine" on screen tells you nothing about whether its alarm CPU is truly independent.
01
🧠
Electronic · Safety Architecture
Dual-CPU Alarm Path
Alarm generation runs on a processor independent from the main trend/display CPU, so a main CPU crash cannot silently disable alarms.
IEC 62304 Class C · IEC 60601-1-8
02
🔒
Software · Patient
Non-Latching High Priority Alarms with Silence Limit
High-priority alarms cannot be permanently silenced; any pause auto-resumes within a bounded time window, preventing an indefinitely muted critical alarm.
IEC 60601-1-8 — silence time limits
03
🎚️
Mechanical · Patient
NIBP Independent Over-Pressure Relief
A hardware pressure-relief valve vents the cuff independently of software control if pressure exceeds a fixed safety threshold.
IEC 80601-2-30 §201.11.2
04
⚡
Electrical · Patient
CF Applied Part Isolation
Every patient-connected parameter module is electrically isolated from mains via optocouplers/isolation transformers, limiting leakage current to ≤10 µA.
IEC 60601-1 Table 1 — CF classification
05
🔋
Electrical · Continuity
Internal Battery for Transport Continuity
Battery autonomy ensures uninterrupted monitoring during transfer between mains supplies — verified with an annual load/capacity test.
IEC 60601-1 · site transport protocol
06
☎️
Electrical · Interlock
Nurse-Call Relay Output
Dry-contact relay signals the ward call system independently of the on-screen alarm, extending notification beyond the bedside.
Verify actuation at every PPM
07
🫀
Software · Patient
Validated Arrhythmia Detection Algorithm
ECG rhythm classification is validated against the ANSI/AAMI EC13 test waveform library before release, and re-verified at PPM.
ANSI/AAMI EC13
08
📒
Software · Forensic
Event Log / Audit Trail
Non-volatile log of alarm events, silences, parameter changes, and technical faults — reviewed at PPM and after any reported incident.
IEC 62304 · MHRA incident investigation
09
📡
Software · System
Central Station "Signal Lost" Alarm
Network or telemetry link dropout triggers its own distinct alarm at the central station, separate from physiological alarms, so a communication gap is never mistaken for a stable patient.
Network redundancy design
§ 06 · Swiss Cheese Model
Defence-in-Depth
Each layer has holes. Harm reaches the patient only when holes align across all layers simultaneously.
I
Inherent Design Controls
CF isolation removes the micro-shock pathway. NIBP over-pressure relief is independent of software. Dual-CPU alarm architecture separates alarm generation from trend/display processing.
CF isolationNIBP overpressure reliefDual-CPU alarm path
II
Automatic Protection Devices
Validated arrhythmia algorithm classifies dangerous rhythms automatically. Signal-lost detection flags a broken telemetry link without waiting for a human to notice.
Arrhythmia algorithmSignal-lost detectionNon-latching high alarms
III
Alarms & Warnings
IEC 60601-1-8 three-priority framework requires clinician attention. This layer fails when alarm fatigue causes silencing or when limits are set too wide to fire at all.
Asystole alarmDesaturation alarmIBP disconnect alarmNurse-call relay
IV
Clinical Procedures & Human Factors
Alarm limit verification at shift handover, cuff-sizing protocol, IBP zeroing protocol, standardised lead-placement diagrams, IEC 62366 usability engineering.
Alarm limit checklistCuff sizing chartIBP zeroing protocolIEC 62366 usability
V
Planned Preventive Maintenance (EBME)
Annual ECG/arrhythmia simulator test, NIBP calibration and over-pressure test, IBP simulator verification, nurse-call relay test, IEC 62353 electrical safety re-test, battery capacity test.
Simulator verificationNIBP calibrationIEC 62353 ESRNurse-call testBattery capacity
VI
Post-Market Surveillance & Vigilance
MHRA Yellow Card and MIR reporting, FSCA response, trend analysis of nuisance-alarm rates across the fleet, ECRI/AAMI hazard alert dissemination.
MHRA Yellow CardDatix incidentFSN responseECRI alerts
